In the previous hour, we have discussed the fight-or-flight syndrome. Let’s think about that again for a moment. This powerful response exists for one purpose: to help protect you from something that is threatening to harm you. All the changes that take place in your body during this process work to produce a high-level burst of energy. This suddenly produced energy helps you get ready to fight hard or run fast. This response has been very helpful to the human race in times of emergency when every second counts and life is on the line.

If the risk, however, is more emotional than physical—say, if you are simply having a disagreement with your spouse or your kids or your boss—the fight or flight response can work against you in a big way. During those times, the fight-or-flight response can interfere with your ability to handle these things sensibly. Instead, your body builds up steam for a big blowup. 

Most inappropriate expressions of anger are the result of this fight-or-flight response getting out of control. You need to find a way to shut it down before that happens. 

Think of shutting down the fight or-flight response this way: You can eliminate those angry outbursts by reducing the pressure that builds up in you. And you reduce the pressure—not by “venting” or “letting off steam”, or by exploding—but by eliminating the source of that pressure. 

That pressure-building process is actually fed by a series of thoughts, actions, and attitudes that keep the emotional “steam” building inside you until you finally erupt in a burst of angry energy. These thought processes and actions fuel your fight-or-flight response until you strike out verbally or physically to defend yourself from a perceived threat. 

Maybe something was said or done that caused you to fear losing esteem or control at home or elsewhere. You respond by blowing your top at your spouse, your boss, or your employees, creating pain, destruction, and confusion for everyone. Your anger and your fight-or-flight mechanism are on the rampage. In order to get your anger under control, you must reduce the pressure by turning off what causes it. 

Many people believe that counseling or therapy is the answer to fixing anger problems. It is true that counseling can sometimes be helpful in addressing the long-term, root causes that allow the anger to get a foothold in the first place. But counseling has one big drawback: it can take months or even years – not to mention lots of money – to fully address these root causes. Because you are taking this course, we do not believe you have that kind of time. You have probably been told that you need to change now or face serious consequences. Don’t worry. There is another option that can be used immediately.

You can begin to get your anger under control right now by taking away the fuel that is feeding your anger fire. If you are like most chronically angry people, you probably think that your anger is triggered by things that happen to you, especially the things that other people do to you and say about you. In reality, much of your anger is driven by those attitudes, thoughts and actions that take place while your fight-or-flight response is building. And even though it sounds too simple to be true, you can start getting your anger under control almost immediately, if you will just stop doing those things you always do when you get angry. 

Maybe you have tried for years to control your anger. If you have tried books and counseling and medication with no real progress to show for it, now it is time to take another approach. 

You can stop feeding that fire inside you if you will commit today to stop doing things that, by themselves or together, are feeding your anger. With that destructive fire under control, we can then focus on making permanent changes in your relationship with anger—changes that can lead to a happier, less crazy life for you and those around you. 

Beginning right now, just STOP.

1. Stop talking.

Silence is the safest action to take when rage boils up inside of us. In the middle of a disagreement or frustrating situation, your first reaction is to hang in there and keep arguing in order to defend yourself. But this just provides more fuel for your fire, and the situation is likely to get out of hand. Don’t feed the fire. Put a sock in it. Shut up, and the fire will die out.

2. Stop hanging around

When you feel anger starting to build inside you, get away as quickly as you can. Don’t wait to see how things turn out or try to get control of the situation. Once your anger level hits 5 on a scale of 10, leave the scene and don’t come back until you—and everyone—else have calmed down. If you don’t tend that anger fire and keep stoking it, it will go out. 

3. Stop glaring

Glaring is a way of issuing a visual challenge to someone. It also provides an automatic fuel boost to your anger fire. Look out the window until you calm down—or down at your shoes. Look anywhere else. Don’t try to start a fight with your eyes.

4. Stop interrupting

Interrupting someone in order to get your own point across or to defend yourself during an argument is another way to stoke that anger fire. If someone interrupts you, let them; don’t try to argue over them. If you are in the habit of interrupting, this will take some practice. Remember Rule One: stop talking. If you can’t do that, move on to Rule Two—leave.

5. Stop using profanity

This isn’t a religious or a moral issue; it is a survival issue! People use profanity to express anger. Those four-letter words are charged with strong emotion, and studies show that they help fuel the fight-or-flight response. If you don’t curse, you won’t be firing up that pressure cooker. Saying “Gee, whiz” or any of a thousand substitutes definitely has a lower anger response than cussing a blue streak. 

6. Stop name-calling

There is really not very much difference between cursing and name-calling. They are both aggressive, insulting and, most of all, they both stoke that anger fire. So stop calling people names—and not just vile, crude names; stop using any term that is an insult or a put-down. Even terms like “stupid” and “crazy” are out. 

7. Stop making threats

“I’m warning you, don’t push me!” Does that sound familiar? Threats—whether subtle or in-your-face—can be terrorizing to people around you. They also add more fuel to the anger rush, giving you the illusion of being tough and in control. Your body prepares to follow up on the threat. Threats stoke your anger fire quickly. 

8. Stop pointing

9. Stop yelling, raising your voice, or talking in a mean tone.

The tone and volume of your voice are quick ways to communicate anger. If you yell, the meaning to the other person is unmistakable. A loud, angry tone of voice also feeds your anger cycle. Start monitoring your tone and volume, using a scale of 0 – 10 (where zero equals silence). Anytime you get above a three, it is time to turn it down. Your loud, angry tone will feed that anger fire faster than anything else.

10. Stop being sarcastic. Stop mocking.

This is a sneaky kind of anger expression, where you can get a dig in at someone and then claim to just be kidding. Just like name-calling, it is disrespectful, hurtful, and mean, and it can cause real damage. Using sarcasm and that mocking tone also feeds your anger. Be nice, be respectful, or be quiet. 

11. Stop throwing things, slamming doors, or banging walls.

Seems like this should be a no-brainer doesn’t it? Nothing starts your anger cycle quicker or keeps it building up faster than flexing those muscles and whacking something. Don’t call it “letting off steam.” You’ve tried it, you know: It actually builds up more steam. 

12. Stop all non-affectionate touching.

Do not, we repeat, do not touch in anger, including any kind of pushing or holding. It is a terrible idea. Bruises evident from “just holding her until she calmed down” can win you a free trip downtown in a police car. Get a grip on your anger and quit grabbing other people—no matter what. 

13. Stop telling hero stories.

Every time you tell someone how you really chewed out somebody or zinged someone good with a cutting remark, you are stoking your inner anger fire. Those hero stories, those “war” stories are just the desperate bragging of an anger junkie looking for a quick fix. Don’t kid yourself—nobody really believes that bull anyway. 

14. Stop sighing, clucking or rolling your eyes.

This is more sneaky anger behavior. Since it doesn’t seem to be openly destructive or mean-spirited—like glaring—you think you can get away with it. But those little actions still clearly signal that you are tired of putting up with someone. It can fuel anger responses both in ourselves and in those around us. Stop it. 

15. Stop “driving” yourself insane.

Anger addicts are generally angry drivers. Impatient, aggressive, rude driving is illegal and unsafe, and no way to prepare yourself for a peaceful destination. Aggressive driving can have your fire well stoked by the time you hit the front door. Changing the way you drive can be an important first step to mellowing out and changing your relationship to anger. 

Follow these common sense rules:

  1. Drive within 5 mph of the speed limit at all times.

  2. Don’t honk the horn in anger.

  3. Don’t run yellow lights.

  4. Be courteous when someone needs to merge or change lanes.

  5. Don’t make critical comments about anyone else’s driving.

  6. Don’t make vulgar gestures or yell at other drivers.

  7. If you get angry while driving, move to a slower lane and calm down.
     

Yes, we agree that there are a lot of idiots on the road. Don’t be one of them.

Be persistent. Be patient.

We understand that trying to eliminate behaviors is very difficult. But no one ever told you it would be easy. Old habits—even destructive ones—are hard to change and difficult to extinguish. As an anger addict, withdrawing from these adrenaline-stoking behaviors will probably be the toughest thing you have ever done. But it can be done, it has been done, and we are confident that with resolve and persistence, you too can get rid of those that fuel your anger once and for all.
